Chapter Four

When Discernment Becomes Suspicion

Having considered the relationship between spiritual gifts and authority, another question naturally follows. What happens when a genuine gift begins operating apart from the character of Christ? Few gifts illustrate this danger more clearly than discernment. Properly understood, discernment is one of God's greatest protections for His people. It enables believers to distinguish truth from error, wisdom from deception, and righteousness from compromise. Yet like every gift entrusted to the Church, discernment can become distorted when humility is replaced by pride.

The purpose of discernment is not to make believers suspicious of one another. Its purpose is to help the Church remain faithful to Christ. Scripture consistently encourages believers to test the spirits, examine teachings, and hold firmly to sound doctrine. These commands are not invitations to become cynical. Rather, they call God's people to exercise wisdom while remaining rooted in love. Discernment without love eventually loses its biblical purpose because it begins searching for faults rather than seeking truth.

One of the subtle dangers of spiritual maturity is the temptation to believe that experience alone guarantees accurate judgment. As believers grow in their knowledge of Scripture and become more aware of false teaching, they naturally become more cautious. Caution, however, is not the same as suspicion. Healthy discernment evaluates evidence through the light of God's Word. Suspicion often reaches conclusions before the evidence has been fully considered. One is patient, while the other is eager to confirm what it already assumes.

This distinction is especially important because suspicion frequently disguises itself as spiritual vigilance. A believer may sincerely think they are protecting the Church while gradually developing a habit of questioning motives that only God can truly know. Every unfamiliar ministry becomes dangerous. Every differing opinion becomes compromise. Every disagreement becomes evidence of deception. Instead of carefully examining what is being taught, the focus shifts toward constantly examining people themselves.

Jesus never ignored error, but neither did He assume the worst about every person He encountered. He confronted hypocrisy with clarity, yet He also extended mercy to those who genuinely sought Him. He distinguished between hardened rebellion and honest misunderstanding. His discernment was always governed by perfect truth and perfect love. He saw every heart completely, yet His responses consistently reflected the wisdom of His Father rather than the impulses of human judgment.

The Pharisees provide one of Scripture's clearest examples of discernment corrupted by pride. They possessed remarkable knowledge of the Law and carefully guarded religious traditions. Yet when Truth Himself stood before them, their confidence in their own spiritual perception prevented them from recognizing the Messiah. Their conclusions had been formed long before they examined the evidence. What they believed to be discernment had become suspicion, and suspicion ultimately blinded them to the very One they claimed to be waiting for.

The same danger exists within the Church today. Believers can become so focused on identifying false teachers that they neglect encouraging faithful servants. They may spend more time exposing error than proclaiming Christ. Conversations become dominated by warnings, controversies, and criticism until fear quietly replaces hope. Discernment that is no longer producing greater love for Christ and His people should cause every believer to pause and examine the condition of their own heart.

None of this suggests that discernment is unnecessary. On the contrary, Scripture repeatedly commands believers to exercise it faithfully. False teaching is real. Spiritual deception is real. Wolves still seek opportunities to scatter the flock. The answer, however, is not suspicion toward everyone. The answer is deeper submission to God's Word, greater dependence upon the Holy Spirit, and a continual commitment to speak the truth in love.

Whenever discernment produces humility, compassion, and greater devotion to Christ, it is accomplishing its intended purpose. When it produces arrogance, isolation, and a constant desire to question the sincerity of others, something has gone wrong. The gift itself has not failed. The heart through which it is operating has begun drifting away from the example of Christ.

Every spiritual gift reflects the character of the One who gives it. Discernment is no exception. It was never intended to become a weapon used against fellow believers. It is a gift entrusted to the Church so that truth may be protected, error corrected, and Christ glorified. When exercised with humility, it strengthens the body of Christ. When governed by pride, it becomes another example of how even God's good gifts can be misused when they are separated from His heart.
