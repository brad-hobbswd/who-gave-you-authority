Chapter One

The Birth of Self Appointed Authority

Authority has never been humanity's greatest problem. The problem has always been the desire to possess authority apart from God. Throughout Scripture, authority is presented as something that belongs to the Lord and is entrusted, never seized. It is given for service rather than status, stewardship rather than superiority, and responsibility rather than recognition. Whenever men or women begin treating authority as something to be acquired instead of something to be received, they step onto dangerous ground.

This distinction lies at the heart of every conflict that has unfolded throughout biblical history. The issue has never simply been leadership. Scripture does not discourage leadership, nor does it diminish those whom God calls to shepherd His people. Rather, it continually warns against the subtle temptation to assume a position that God has neither assigned nor affirmed. Self appointed authority is born whenever the heart begins believing that personal conviction, experience, gifting, or influence grants permission to speak and act with God's authority.

Such thinking rarely develops overnight. It begins quietly, often unnoticed, because it disguises itself beneath admirable qualities. A person may possess extensive biblical knowledge, years of ministry experience, remarkable spiritual gifts, or sincere passion for truth. None of these qualities are wrong in themselves. In fact, each may be evidence of God's grace working within a believer's life. Yet none of them, individually or collectively, establish divine authority. Gifts reveal the generosity of God. Authority reveals His appointment.

The distinction is easily overlooked because we naturally associate ability with qualification. We assume that someone who communicates well must therefore possess unquestionable wisdom. We equate confidence with certainty and visibility with spiritual maturity. Modern culture rewards those who gather influence, and the Church is not immune from that temptation. Platforms grow, voices become recognizable, opinions spread rapidly, and before long a person's reputation may carry more weight than the Scriptures they claim to defend.

This tendency is not unique to the present generation. It has existed wherever people have mistaken proximity to God for equality with God. The desire to elevate oneself above the place assigned by the Lord reaches back to the earliest pages of Scripture, revealing that the misuse of authority is not primarily an organizational problem or a denominational problem. It is a problem of the human heart.

The first rebellion did not begin on earth. Before sin entered humanity, pride had already found its place within one of God's created beings. Lucifer was not dissatisfied because God had failed him. Scripture presents him as one who had been given beauty, responsibility, and honor. His fall began when gratitude gave way to ambition and stewardship gave way to self exaltation. Isaiah records the repeated declaration, "I will," exposing the true nature of his rebellion. His desire was not simply to possess greater influence but to occupy a place that belonged to God alone.

That pattern has never disappeared. Self appointed authority always begins by shifting attention away from the One who grants authority and toward the one who desires to possess it. Pride persuades the heart that it deserves more than it has been given, while humility remains content to serve faithfully wherever God has placed it. The conflict, therefore, is not merely between authority and rebellion but between surrender and self exaltation.

This same temptation entered the Garden of Eden. The serpent did not begin by denying God's existence. He questioned God's word, challenged His goodness, and suggested that Adam and Eve could obtain something God had intentionally withheld. The promise was not simply knowledge but elevation. Humanity was enticed to believe that fulfillment could be found by reaching beyond the boundaries established by the Creator. Sin entered the world because mankind desired what God had not entrusted to them on His terms.

From that moment forward, Scripture repeatedly reveals the consequences of assuming what God has not given. Cain rejected God's instruction and determined that his own way was sufficient. Korah challenged the leadership God had established through Moses. King Saul gradually replaced obedience with personal judgment, convincing himself that partial obedience was acceptable when it appeared beneficial. The Pharisees elevated tradition above truth and eventually spoke with an authority that neither reflected nor submitted to the heart of God. Though each account unfolds in different circumstances, the root remains the same. Human beings continually attempt to establish themselves where only God has the right to appoint.

The danger becomes even greater within the Church because spiritual language can easily conceal spiritual pride. A person may sincerely believe they are defending truth while gradually placing their own conclusions beyond examination. They may begin speaking as though disagreement with them is equivalent to disagreement with God. Over time, correction becomes domination, conviction becomes control, and confidence becomes an unwillingness to be taught. What began as a sincere desire to serve Christ quietly transforms into a desire to preserve personal influence.

This is why Scripture consistently places character before position. God is never impressed by gifts that operate independently of humility. Knowledge without love produces arrogance, leadership without servanthood produces oppression, and influence without submission eventually creates division. The Kingdom of God measures greatness very differently from the kingdoms of this world. Before God entrusts greater responsibility, He first forms the heart of the servant.

Self appointed authority, therefore, is not simply a leadership issue. It is a worship issue. It reveals itself whenever the heart begins seeking significance apart from complete dependence upon God. Until that pursuit is confronted, every title, platform, gift, or opportunity becomes vulnerable to misuse. The birth of self appointed authority is not marked by public recognition or visible promotion. It begins in the quiet decisions of the heart, where surrender slowly gives way to self importance and stewardship is exchanged for ownership.
