Chapter Twelve

Returning the Throne to Jesus

Every chapter of this book has led to this moment. We began by examining the birth of self appointed authority and followed its destructive path through pride, manipulation, misplaced confidence, and the pursuit of personal kingdoms. Along the way we discovered that gifts cannot replace character, influence cannot substitute for humility, and authority divorced from Christ eventually becomes dangerous to both the one who exercises it and those who submit to it. Every lesson has pointed toward a single conclusion. The throne was never meant for us. It has always belonged to Jesus Christ.

The greatest problem facing the Church is not merely the existence of false authority. It is the subtle tendency of every human heart to compete for a place that belongs to God alone. Pride does not always announce itself with obvious rebellion. More often it appears through quiet self reliance, the desire for recognition, or the assumption that our opinions deserve unquestioned acceptance. Left unchecked, these attitudes slowly move Christ from the center until our own ambitions occupy the place reserved for Him.

Returning the throne to Jesus begins with repentance. Repentance is far more than acknowledging individual failures. It is the intentional surrender of every area where we have attempted to rule apart from Him. We relinquish the need to control outcomes, defend our reputation, preserve our influence, or prove ourselves superior to others. We lay every title, every accomplishment, every gift, and every ambition at His feet because none of them were ever ours to possess independently. Everything we have has been entrusted to us by His grace.

This surrender transforms the way we view ministry. We no longer labor to establish our own legacy but to advance the Kingdom of God. Success is no longer measured by numbers, applause, or public recognition. Instead, faithfulness becomes the defining standard. A quiet act of obedience carries greater eternal significance than a celebrated accomplishment pursued for selfish motives. Heaven measures differently because heaven values the heart before the achievement.

Returning the throne to Jesus also restores unity within the Body of Christ. Competition gives way to cooperation. Envy is replaced by gratitude. Believers rejoice when others are used by God because they recognize that every genuine work of the Spirit glorifies the same Savior. Ministries cease functioning as rival kingdoms and instead become fellow laborers serving under one King. Diversity of gifts no longer produces division because every gift exists for the common good of Christ's Church.

This perspective also reshapes leadership. Those entrusted with authority understand that they are stewards rather than owners. The Church belongs to Christ. The gospel belongs to Christ. The truth belongs to Christ. Even the people we shepherd ultimately belong to Christ. We are servants caring for what has been entrusted to us for a season, knowing that one day we will give an account to the Chief Shepherd. Such knowledge produces humility rather than fear because our confidence rests in His faithfulness rather than our own ability.

The believer who returns the throne to Jesus discovers remarkable freedom. There is no longer a need to defend personal kingdoms because none are being built. There is no need to compete for significance because our identity is secure in Christ. There is no pressure to appear perfect because His grace is sufficient. We become free to repent quickly, forgive generously, serve joyfully, and love sacrificially. The burden of self promotion is replaced by the peace that comes from belonging entirely to Him.

This truth reaches beyond church leadership into every area of life. In our homes, workplaces, friendships, and communities, Christ must remain enthroned. Every decision should reflect His character. Every conversation should reveal His love. Every opportunity should point others toward His saving grace. Authority exercised under the lordship of Jesus becomes a means through which His compassion, justice, mercy, and truth are made visible in the world.

Ultimately, the question posed by this book has never been directed primarily toward pastors, teachers, or church leaders. It has been directed toward every believer who has ever been tempted to place confidence in themselves rather than in God. "Who gave you authority?" is not merely a question about leadership. It is an invitation to examine the condition of our own hearts. Have we submitted ourselves completely to Christ, or have we quietly assumed ownership of what only He has the right to rule?

There is only one rightful King. There is only one flawless Shepherd. There is only one perfect Judge. There is only one Head of the Church. His authority is not borrowed, temporary, or delegated from another because all authority in heaven and earth belongs to Him. Every human authority is temporary. Every earthly platform will one day disappear. Every title will eventually be forgotten. Jesus Christ alone remains forever.

As you close this book, my prayer is not that you remember its chapters, its arguments, or even its author. My prayer is that your eyes are lifted higher than every personality, every ministry, and every human voice until they rest upon Jesus Christ alone. May every gift you possess be surrendered to Him. May every opportunity you receive glorify Him. May every measure of authority entrusted to you reflect His humility, His holiness, and His love. If this book accomplishes anything of lasting value, let it be this: that every throne within our hearts is emptied of self and occupied once again by the only One worthy to reign.
