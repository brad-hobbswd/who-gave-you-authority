Chapter Three

Gifts Are Not Credentials

Having established that true authority is revealed through humility rather than position, an important question naturally follows. If authority is not measured by influence, titles, or public recognition, then what role do spiritual gifts play? Throughout the history of the Church, this question has often been answered incorrectly. Many sincere believers have unintentionally equated gifting with authority, assuming that visible demonstrations of God's power automatically validate every opinion, decision, or action of the individual through whom those gifts are expressed. Scripture, however, draws a careful distinction between what God gives and whom God appoints.

Spiritual gifts are acts of divine grace. They are never presented as rewards for maturity, nor are they evidence that someone has reached the highest level of spiritual growth. They are entrusted by the Holy Spirit for the edification of the body of Christ, enabling believers to serve one another according to God's purpose. Their source is the grace of God rather than the merit of the recipient. For that reason alone, no gift should ever become the foundation upon which personal authority is built.

The apostle Paul devoted considerable attention to this subject because the church at Corinth had begun valuing spectacular gifts while neglecting spiritual maturity. Their gatherings displayed remarkable manifestations of the Spirit, yet they were also marked by division, jealousy, immorality, and pride. Paul's response was not to deny the gifts, nor to discourage their use. Instead, he reminded the believers that gifts exercised without love had lost their intended purpose. His famous discourse on charity was not inserted randomly into the discussion. It was deliberately placed between his teaching on spiritual gifts to demonstrate that character must always govern the exercise of every gift God gives.

This principle remains just as necessary today. A gifted communicator may preach with remarkable clarity while privately neglecting humility. Someone may possess extraordinary discernment and yet struggle to extend mercy. Another may demonstrate leadership that builds large ministries while quietly allowing ambition to replace servanthood. None of these realities invalidate the gifts themselves, but they remind us that gifts and character do not automatically mature at the same pace. God may use an individual to accomplish His purposes while simultaneously calling that individual to greater holiness and surrender.

Confusion arises when believers begin treating giftedness as unquestionable authority. Instead of testing every teaching against Scripture, they accept conclusions because of the reputation of the speaker. Instead of examining fruit, they become captivated by talent. Instead of discerning character over time, they are persuaded by charisma in the moment. This creates an environment where personality gradually overshadows biblical truth, and influence becomes the measure of credibility.

Jesus never encouraged such thinking. Those who followed Him were continually directed back to obedience, humility, and faithfulness rather than outward displays of greatness. He warned that many would perform impressive works in His name while never truly knowing Him. His words remind the Church that miraculous activity alone cannot become the measure of authentic spiritual authority. The presence of a gift does not eliminate the necessity of examining the life through which that gift is exercised.

The Church flourishes when gifts are received with gratitude and exercised with humility. Every gift has a purpose, and every member of the body contributes something necessary to the health of the whole. Problems arise only when gifts become identities instead of ministries. A teacher is first a disciple before becoming a teacher. A pastor remains a servant before becoming a shepherd. A prophet remains accountable to God's Word before speaking to God's people. Every calling rests upon the same foundation of submission to Christ.

When gifts become credentials, humility slowly gives way to entitlement. Correction becomes difficult because reputation is mistaken for infallibility. Accountability begins to disappear, and the individual may eventually believe that their gifting places them beyond examination. History repeatedly demonstrates the dangers of such thinking. Many public failures have not begun with a loss of gifting but with a gradual abandonment of humility.

The safest place for every believer is to remember that gifts belong to God. They are entrusted, not possessed. They are opportunities to serve, not platforms upon which to elevate ourselves. The greater the gift, the greater the responsibility to walk in humility, because every gift ultimately exists to glorify Christ rather than the one through whom it is expressed.
