Chapter Six

Building Personal Kingdoms

When spiritual gifts are used to establish personal influence rather than glorify Christ, an inevitable shift begins to take place. The ministry that once existed to serve others slowly becomes centered upon preserving itself. What began as a calling gradually becomes an identity. Success becomes measured by visibility instead of faithfulness, and influence quietly replaces obedience as the standard by which effectiveness is judged. This transformation rarely happens intentionally. It develops one decision at a time until the work of God is no longer distinguished from the reputation of the one leading it.

The temptation to build personal kingdoms has existed throughout every generation of God's people. From the tower of Babel to the religious leaders of Jesus' day, Scripture repeatedly exposes humanity's desire to make a name for itself. The builders of Babel were not simply constructing a city. They openly declared their intention to establish a name that would endure apart from complete dependence upon God. Their project represented more than architecture. It revealed the ambition of the human heart to secure significance through its own achievements.

That same desire can quietly find its way into Christian ministry. Churches may become identified more by personalities than by Christ. Ministries may become known for their leaders rather than for the gospel they proclaim. Platforms, conferences, publications, podcasts, and social media can all become useful instruments for advancing the Kingdom of God, yet they can also become subtle opportunities for self promotion when the heart begins seeking recognition instead of service.

There is nothing inherently wrong with influence. Throughout Scripture, God entrusted influence to men and women who faithfully served His purposes. Joseph influenced Egypt. Daniel influenced Babylon. Esther influenced an empire. Paul influenced churches across the Roman world. Influence itself is not the danger. The danger arises when influence becomes something we protect more carefully than our relationship with God. At that moment, preserving our position begins taking precedence over pursuing His will.

Jesus consistently resisted every opportunity to build a kingdom centered upon Himself in the way people expected. After feeding the multitudes, the crowds sought to make Him king by force. Rather than accepting their admiration, He withdrew from them. Again and again He refused to manipulate public opinion or capitalize on popularity. His mission was never to gather followers for His own reputation but to accomplish the Father's will. Even His miracles pointed beyond Himself, revealing the glory of God rather than satisfying human curiosity.

The contrast between Christ and the religious leaders of His day could not have been greater. Jesus rebuked those who loved the chief seats, sought public recognition, and desired the praise of men more than the approval of God. Their religious activity appeared impressive, but beneath the outward appearance lay hearts consumed with status. They had confused visibility with faithfulness and applause with obedience.

The modern Church faces many of the same temptations. Numbers can become more important than discipleship. Influence may become more valuable than integrity. Ministries may become protective of their image, reluctant to acknowledge weakness, and resistant to correction. In such environments, preserving the organization gradually overshadows pursuing the heart of Christ. The ministry may continue appearing successful while quietly drifting away from the simplicity of the gospel.

The apostle Paul demonstrated an entirely different perspective. Although entrusted with remarkable influence throughout the early Church, he continually directed attention away from himself and toward Christ. He reminded believers that one plants, another waters, but God alone gives the increase. His confidence rested not in his own abilities but in the faithfulness of the One who had called him. Paul's ministry flourished precisely because he understood that it did not belong to him.

Every servant of Christ must eventually answer an important question. Are we building something that depends upon our name, or are we investing in a Kingdom that will endure long after our names are forgotten? Human kingdoms require constant protection because they are built upon temporary foundations. The Kingdom of God requires faithful stewardship because it already belongs to the King. Our responsibility is not to establish our own legacy but to remain faithful to the One whose Kingdom cannot be shaken.

Whenever Christ becomes the center of our work, success is measured differently. Faithfulness becomes more valuable than popularity. Obedience becomes more important than recognition. Humility becomes more desirable than prominence. The goal is no longer to leave behind monuments to ourselves but to point every generation toward Jesus Christ. Only then can we be certain that we are building His Kingdom rather than quietly constructing one of our own.

Yet building personal kingdoms creates another danger that is not immediately obvious. Once our identity becomes tied to the work we have built, correction begins to feel like a personal attack. We become invested not only in what we believe but also in preserving the image we have created. That is where many leaders begin the slow transition from humble servants to unquestioned authorities. The issue is no longer simply what is true. It becomes whether we are willing to admit that we could be wrong. That question leads naturally to the next chapter, where we must examine the subtle pride hidden behind the words, "Who told you that you were right?"
